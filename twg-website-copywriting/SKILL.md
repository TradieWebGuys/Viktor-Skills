# TWG Trade Client Copywriting

Conversion-focused, SEO and AIO-ready website copy for trade businesses.

This skill is designed to produce pages that read naturally, convert real buyers, and still give search engines and AI answer engines the structure they need.

---

## How To Use These Files

Read the file that matches the page type being written.

- **Service pages:** `service-pages.md`
- **Location pages:** `location-pages.md`
- **Homepage:** `homepage.md`
- **About page:** `about-page.md`
- **Contact page:** `contact-page.md`
- **Client details:** `client-kb.md`

Always read `client-kb.md` before writing client copy. It contains the client's business name, locations, service mix, licences, USPs, CTA preferences, restricted claims, contact details, and tone rules. This skill does not define or create that file — it is produced and owned by the client onboarding process. Read it, never write to it from here.

Do not assume details from a previous client. Use the current client KB, the current brief, and any uploaded reference material.

## Guardrails

- Output is a draft for the project manager to review before the copy goes to the website. This skill does not publish content directly.
- Always read `client-kb.md` before writing. If it does not exist for this client, ask rather than guessing client details.
- Always check the client's keyword research Google Sheet before writing. If it does not exist, check with the user before proceeding.
- Pause on missing inputs. If the client name, page type, or primary keyword is not provided, ask rather than guessing.
- Never fabricate claims about pricing, response times, guarantees, equipment, reviews, or accreditations. If a claim is useful but unconfirmed, flag it in a Proofreading Note.
- Keep internal-only material (strategy notes, competitor analysis) out of deliverable copy.
- Writing: plain, direct, active sentences. Apply the anti-AI writing guide. Avoid generic filler (delve, leverage, robust, seamless, unlock, streamline, elevate).

## Priority Order

Apply these priorities in order.

1. **Writing quality** — every sentence must sound natural and help the reader move forward.
2. **Conversion strength** — the page must answer buyer questions, reduce uncertainty, and build confidence.
3. **Differentiation** — the page must include detail that could not sit unchanged on a competitor's website.
4. **SEO and AIO structure** — the page must be easy for search engines and AI answer engines to understand.
5. **Compliance with formatting rules** — metadata, headings, CTAs, FAQs, links, and notes must be complete.

A page that technically follows the checklist but reads like a template has failed.

## Core Principles

- Use UK/Australian English spelling and grammar.
- Write in third person. Use the business name for first mentions and key conviction moments — opening paragraph, trust module, FAQ answers, and close. "We" and "our" are acceptable for subsequent references within a section to avoid mechanical repetition. The page should not read as a personal blog, but natural service page voice mixes both.
- Keep the copy evergreen. Avoid owner-name dependency unless the owner is central to the brand.
- Lead with service capability, buyer confidence, and proof before heavy education.
- Make every section useful on its own.
- Write for the buyer's decision, not just for keyword coverage.
- Use clear, practical language. Avoid inflated agency language.
- Keep service pages commercially focused. Education supports the sale, but it should not take over the page.

### Restricted Language

Follow the user's global restricted phrase list and the client-specific restricted wording in `client-kb.md`.

Also avoid:

- Empty claims such as "committed to excellence" or "customer satisfaction is our priority"
- Generic filler such as "when it comes to" or "whether you're dealing with"
- Vague value statements that are not backed by a specific detail
- Repeated sentence patterns that make the copy sound machine-written
- Overuse of the business name, service keyword, or location
- Technical wording before the reader has been properly oriented
- Unverified claims about pricing, response times, guarantees, equipment, reviews, or accreditations

No emojis in website copy, CTAs, headings, bullets, metadata, or FAQs.

Writing quality: `anti-ai-writing.md` — apply banned vocabulary, specificity rules, negative parallelism ban, and AI pattern checks to all copy. Where this file conflicts with the skill's voice rules (third person, Australian English, client brand tone), the skill rules take precedence.

## Writing Quality Rules

### Reader-First Opening

The first sentence should meet the reader where they are. Start with the problem, decision, risk, or desired outcome. Save technical detail for the second or third sentence.

Weak:

> RCD-protected switchboards are required for compliant electrical safety systems.

Stronger:

> An older switchboard can look harmless from the outside while leaving the property without the safety protection modern homes rely on.

Technical details still matter. They land better after the reader understands why they matter.

For compliance, safety, upgrade, and installation pages, do not confuse serious with alarming.

The opening should make the reader feel guided, not overwhelmed. Avoid leading with a dense risk statement, technical fault chain, or regulation-heavy sentence.

Use this order:

1. Plain buyer concern
2. Service and location
3. One credibility or compliance detail
4. CTA

Do not introduce more than one technical detail in the opening paragraph unless the audience is highly technical.

### Natural Keyword Use

Keywords must fit the sentence. Never bend the sentence around the keyword.

A location mention must carry weight. Use it when it adds context, such as local housing age, coastal exposure, council rules, common infrastructure, climate, property type, or supply authority requirements.

Do not force the location into every H2. A page can be locally strong through the H1, metadata, URL, opening, proof, service area copy, internal links, and specific local detail.

**Location Hierarchy For Local Service Pages**

- Primary location is the strongest location signal when the page targets one suburb or town
- Wider regions and nearby suburbs support the primary location without competing with it

### Earned Detail In Every Main Section

Each main section must include at least one specific detail, such as:

- A method or diagnostic step
- A material or system type
- A compliance requirement
- A timeframe
- A tool or inspection method
- A local condition
- A property type
- A warranty, licence, or accreditation

Generic copy does not pass unless it contains a specific detail the reader can use.

### Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length and structure. Avoid three similar sentences in a row. A good paragraph usually includes one short sentence, one more detailed sentence, and one sentence that moves the reader to the next point.

### Section Openers

The first sentence of a section must not simply repeat the heading. It should answer the implied question behind the heading or give the most useful point first.

Weak:

> Our switchboard upgrade services cover a full range of switchboard upgrade work.

Stronger:

> The safest upgrade depends on the board's age, the number of circuits, and whether the property needs supply-side work through the local network.

### Closing Lines

The last sentence before a CTA should be one of the strongest sentences in the section. It should reinforce value, reduce doubt, or create a clear reason to act.

Avoid generic finishes such as "the team is here to help".

### Tight Trust Sections

Why Choose sections should build confidence quickly, not slow the page down.

Use short, scannable benefit bullets. The bold lead-in should carry the main point, and the explanation should be brief.

Avoid giving every USP a full sentence unless the point needs clarification for the buyer. If the section starts reading like five small paragraphs, tighten it.

## Conversion Logic For Service Pages

Most service pages should not open with a long list of warning signs. The reader usually wants to know:

1. Can this business handle the job?
2. Why should I trust them?
3. Is there proof?
4. Does this apply to my situation?
5. What should I do next?

For most planned, compliance, upgrade, installation, and replacement services, capability should appear before self-identification.

For emergency or highly reactive services, self-identification can appear earlier because the reader arrives with a symptom and needs confirmation quickly.

Use `service-pages.md` for page-order rules by buyer state.

## Proof And Asset Rules

Proof should appear on most commercial service pages.

Use one or more of the following:

- Review widget placeholder
- Short testimonial placeholder
- Project gallery placeholder
- Before and after image placeholder
- Case study snippet
- Certification or compliance proof
- Review count or rating, only when verified

A proof module can be included as a placeholder when the website build will supply the live asset later.

### Conditional Visual Proof

Before and after or gallery placeholders should only appear when the service has a visible outcome that helps the buyer decide.

Good fit:

- Renovations
- New builds
- Roofing work
- Solar installations
- Switchboard upgrades
- EV charger installations
- Landscaping
- Concrete work
- Bathroom, kitchen, laundry, and outdoor transformations

Poor fit unless there is a specific reason:

- Fault finding
- Gas compliance
- Leak detection
- Backflow testing
- Minor repairs
- Emergency attendance
- Diagnostic inspections

Use visual modules only where they improve confidence. Do not add them for decoration.

---

## Decision Support Instead Of Mandatory Pricing

Do not make pricing or objections mandatory on every service page.

Use a Decision Support Module when it helps the reader make a better choice. Pricing can appear inside this module only when it genuinely helps.

Examples:

- Is it worth replacing an old switchboard?
- Should you repair or replace the hot water system?
- Do you need solar batteries with the new system?
- Is pipe relining better than excavation?
- What affects the cost of a roof replacement?
- What should landlords know before booking safety work?

If a cost section appears, it must be reader-helpful. Explain what affects price, what the quote includes, and how the client confirms the scope. Do not invent price ranges. Do not use dollar figures or ranges unless the client has confirmed fixed pricing in writing — for variable-scope trade work, the only correct framing is what affects the quote, not what it costs.

This applies to FAQs as well as body copy. A cost FAQ should explain how the reader gets a confirmed price, not attempt to provide one.

## CTA Guidelines

Use CTA wording and contact details from `client-kb.md`.

- CTA wording should match the reader's stage of decision.
- Vary CTAs across the page.
- Do not paste the same CTA repeatedly.
- Do not embed the phone number in ordinary body sentences — it appears in the CTA element only.
- CTA frequency should support the page flow without making the page feel interrupted.

Recommended CTA moments:

1. After the opening
2. After capability is clear
3. After trust and proof are established
4. After a useful decision-support or process section
5. After the final close

Not every page needs the maximum number of CTAs. Use enough to make action easy without making the page feel noisy.

### CTA Intent And Channel

Match the CTA to the reader's decision stage.

Use phone CTAs for urgent, emergency, or high-intent readers.

Use form or enquiry CTAs for cautious, educational, compliance, planning, upgrade, or higher-cost services where the reader may want to ask questions, send details, or request a written quote before calling.

Where the client supports enquiries, include at least one form-based CTA variation on non-emergency service pages.

Do not rely only on phone CTAs unless the client has specified phone-only enquiries.

### CTA Structure — Two-Line Button Format

CTAs are conversion buttons, not sentences. Every CTA element must be built as two short, separate lines — never one bolded run-on sentence.

- **Line 1 — the hook:** a short question or statement, max 5–6 words, that reflects the moment in the page the reader just finished. Not bolded.
- **Line 2 — the action:** the button itself. Bold, max 1–3 words plus the number if phone-based. This is the only line that carries the phone number or action label.

**Correct:**
```
Need it fixed today?
**CALL 0431 875 044**
```

**Incorrect (do not do this):**
```
**Call AR Plumbing Service on 0431 875 044, or request a callback to arrange a time that suits.**
```

Rules:

- Never combine the hook and the action into a single bolded sentence.
- The action line is the number alone (`**CALL [number]**`), or a short label (`**ENQUIRE NOW**`, `**REQUEST A QUOTE**`, `**SEND AN ENQUIRY**`) for form-based clients — never a sentence describing what will happen when clicked.
- Do not use "or" to offer two actions in the same button line (e.g. "call or enquire online") — if a client wants both a call and a form option, present them as two separate two-line CTAs stacked together, not merged into one.
- The hook line changes at every placement to match the reader's stage; the action line stays consistent in format throughout the page.

**CTA format examples (adapt to client):**

- Phone only: hook line + `**CALL [number]**`
- Phone, no hook (sparing use — e.g. final close on short pages): `**CALL [number]**` alone
- Form: hook line + `**ENQUIRE NOW**`, `**REQUEST A QUOTE**`, or `**SEND AN ENQUIRY**`
- Dual CTA (client wants both call and form): two stacked two-line CTAs — never merged into one line

## SEO And AIO Rules

Service copy must be readable first and optimised second.

### Required SEO Elements

- Primary keyword in meta title
- Primary keyword in meta description
- Primary keyword in H1
- Primary keyword or close variant in the opening paragraph
- Primary keyword or close variant in at least one natural H2
- FAQ heading includes the service topic
- Internal links use descriptive anchors
- Page includes business name, service, and location in natural places

### Local SEO Without Overuse

Use location signals across the page, not in every heading.

Good location signals include:

- URL slug
- Meta title and description
- H1
- Opening paragraph
- Service area paragraph or module
- Review or project proof from the area
- Internal links to related local services
- Local network, council, climate, housing, or property-type details where relevant

A heading should include the location only when it helps the reader.

### AIO Structure

Write sections so AI answer engines can extract useful snippets.

- Use direct section openers
- Keep paragraphs self-contained
- Include factual, citable details where confirmed
- Use FAQ questions that match real buyer questions
- Avoid claims that sound impressive but cannot be verified

## Internal Linking

Include an internal links table at the foot of every deliverable.

Recommended quantities:

| Word Count | Internal Links |
|---|---|
| 0-500 | 2-3 |
| 500-800 | 3-5 |
| 800-1,200 | 5-7 |
| 1,200+ | 7-10 |

Use descriptive anchor text that tells the reader what the linked page is about.

Default deliverable format depends on the user's request.

- Use tables for metadata, notes, and internal link suggestions.

**For markdown deliverables:**

- Use clean markdown headings.
- Keep metadata in a table.
- Use clear placeholder labels for website assets.
- Keep notes separate from public page copy.

## Batch Writing Protocol

For two or more pages in one batch:

1. Write the first page as the quality benchmark.
2. Create a differentiation plan before writing the rest.
3. Vary opening angle, proof type, heading style, local detail, and section order where appropriate.
4. Do not turn the first page into a template.
5. Run the Quality Audit and Proofreading Pass before moving to the next page.

Run this before delivering any page.

### Writing Quality

- [ ] Opening starts in reader language, not technical language
- [ ] Opening creates confidence before introducing technical risk
- [ ] Opening contains no more than one technical or compliance detail
- [ ] Sentence rhythm varies within paragraphs
- [ ] No section opener repeats the heading
- [ ] Every main section includes earned detail
- [ ] Keyword use feels natural
- [ ] Location mentions add context rather than noise
- [ ] No generic filler or inflated claims
- [ ] Closing lines are strong before CTAs
- [ ] Page does not read like a template
- [ ] Business name is not overused — "we/our" handles subsequent references within sections

### Conversion Strength

- [ ] Capability appears early enough for the buyer state
- [ ] Why choose section uses scannable benefit points where suitable
- [ ] Why Choose bullets are short, scannable, and not mini paragraphs
- [ ] Most trust bullets fit on one line where practical
- [ ] Proof module is included or a reason for omission is clear
- [ ] Review/testimonial/widget placeholder appears where assets are expected
- [ ] Visual proof is included only if relevant
- [ ] Decision-support content helps the reader decide
- [ ] CTAs appear at logical points and vary naturally
- [ ] CTA mix includes a form/enquiry option where the service is not urgent

### CTA Format

- [ ] Every CTA is a two-line hook + button — no CTA is a single bolded run-on sentence
- [ ] No CTA button line uses "or" to merge two actions into one
- [ ] CTA hook wording varies at each placement; no identical CTA back-to-back

### SEO And AIO

- [ ] Primary keyword appears in metadata, H1, opening, and at least one natural H2
- [ ] Location signals are present without heading stuffing
- [ ] FAQ heading includes the service topic
- [ ] FAQ answers are short, standalone, and useful
- [ ] Internal links are included
- [ ] Citable claims are verified or flagged
- [ ] Schema-ready details are present where available

## Proofreading Pass

Read the page as a senior editor reviewing someone else's draft.

Look for:

- Paragraphs that educate without moving the sale forward
- Sections that sound technically correct but commercially flat
- Overloaded headings with repeated service and location phrases
- Weak trust sections that should be scannable benefits
- Proof opportunities that have been missed
- Pricing sections that exist only because a framework demanded them
- Self-identification sections placed too early for the buyer state
- Visual placeholders used where they do not help
- FAQs that repeat the body copy rather than covering extra buyer questions

## Proofreading Notes

Add a brief Proofreading Note only when the user or client needs to review something before publishing.

Use notes for:

- Unverified USPs
- Unverified response times
- Unverified equipment or methods
- Unverified review counts or ratings
- Claims retained because they are strategically useful but need client approval
- Placeholders that require developer or client assets

If no note is needed, do not add one.
